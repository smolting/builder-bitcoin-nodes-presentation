So what if the bitcoin network looks like this? We have our wallets and miners, and they all connect to this big central bitcoin authority.

This thankfully is not how bitcoin works, thankfully for a few reasons:

  • Firstly, if we're just going to defer to a big central authority to manage the ledger why even have miners? Why not make it the issuer of currency as well? You're already trusting
  • Secondly, Satoshi Nakamoto was actually trying to solve the problem of central authority. The cryptographic activists who preceded him already conceded that a decentralized network was desirable, but extremely difficult. Why would the cypherpunks discourage central authority? If that entity ever gets removed, either by incompetence (like the digital currency company going bankrupt), corruption, or by force (government doesn't like competition).

Why a centralized bitcoin sucks: It's not trustless

It's entirely corruptible. (censorship, deplatforming, spying, supply manipulation, etc...)

It's not secure.

There's no chance of being private.

And the amount of resources necessary to run this central entity ensures that you can't feasibly run it on your own. YOu'll a lot of money and infrastructure, meaning you'll need to form a company, drum up investors, etc...basically it's a shitcoin.

So clearly the goal here is to be decentralized. Let's see what happens when we break up this centralized power into little grumpy bitcoins.

You can see that they are all connected to each other, either directly or indirectly. That means they can share information they see. Information like transactions and blocks.

So now let's re-introduce our wallets and miners, and instead of connecting to a central authority, they choose a little grumpy bitcoin to connect to.

And the big reveal here is that the little grumpy bitcoins are actually nodes.

What does a node do?

  • Verifies everything it's told (trustless)
  • Stores all (or at least the last X blocks) of blockchain data
  • Keeps track of all the pending transactions across the network (for fee estimation)
  • Helps new nodes "catch up" with the history and the current state of the network
  • Helps wallets "catch up" with the history of their transactions
  • Helps miners know which transactions they should be putting into the next block

What a node does:

  • Relays
  • Verifies
  • Stores
  • Tracks pending transactions
  • ...to service nodes, wallets, and miners
  • You can be sure that your wallet is getting accurate information
  • You can be sure that your transactions are being relayed to the network
  • You can be sure that your transactions are being included in blocks
  • You can be sure that your transactions are being verified by the network
  • You can be sure that you are not relying on a third party to do any of this for you
